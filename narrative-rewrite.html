Rewriting Your Life Story

You are not just the reader of your story. You are the author.

We live by narratives. They weave past, present, and future into a sense of self. Two people can share an event and remember it entirely differently — and the narrative we carry can lift us, or hold us back.

"I used to avoid walking in the rain. I thought of Gene Kelly and created a new story — smiling as I walked, humming Singing in the Rain. It sounds simple, but it changed how I felt. Small tweaks can shift experience." — Ali

This tool guides you through a gentle rewrite of one narrative that feels unhelpful or heavy. It might be rooted in something that happened, or it might be something you have simply told yourself so many times it has become part of how you see yourself. Both are worth examining. You can make a small edit or a bigger rewrite — whatever serves your well-being.

1

Find the Narrative

Before we can rewrite anything, we need to locate it. What is the narrative — the story or belief — you are bringing to this tool today?

A narrative can be something that happened and still colours how you see yourself. It can also be something you have told yourself so many times — about who you are, what you are capable of, or what you deserve — that it has quietly become a belief. Both are narratives. Both can be rewritten.

For example — Margaret's narrative

"I always put other people first and then end up feeling invisible. It happens at family dinners, in conversations with friends, even at work. I have been telling myself this for years — that speaking up is not worth it because nobody really listens to me anyway."

Write it in your own words, as specifically as you can. It might be a phrase you catch yourself repeating, or a belief that quietly shapes how you behave.
Is it triggered by a particular situation, a person, a type of conversation, a time of day, or a feeling?
Is it your own voice? A critical inner voice that sounds like you but harsher? Or does it carry the tone of someone else — a parent, a teacher, an old relationship?
2

The Scene

Is there a specific moment behind this narrative?
This step is optional. Some narratives are rooted in a specific event — something that happened that you still carry. Others are patterns repeated so many times they have become part of how you see yourself, with no single moment to point to. If there is a particular event behind yours, describe it below in plain facts. If not, move straight on — simply click Next.
For example — Margaret's scene

"At my sister's birthday dinner last year, I started to share something important to me. Two people talked over me and my sister changed the subject. I went quiet for the rest of the evening."

Facts only, no interpretation yet. Not how it felt — just what occurred.
3

The Current Narrative

Capture the narrative you are currently carrying — and how heavily it sits with you.
For example — Margaret's current narrative

"Nobody really listens to me. Speaking up is not worth it — I will only be talked over or ignored."

Try to write it as one or two sentences — the core belief or story you carry about this.

4

Evidence Scan

Look at the narrative from both sides. What supports it — and what softens or contradicts it?
For example — Margaret's evidence scan

Supports: "At the dinner I was talked over. My manager rarely asks for my opinion in meetings."

Softens: "My friend Sarah always asks what I think. I spoke up at book group last month and people listened. My colleague James was in the same situation — he kept speaking up anyway, and people did start to hear him."

Supports the narrative

Softens or contradicts it

Look in your own experience: moments of being heard, resilience shown, times things went better than the narrative suggests. They count even if they feel minor.

Look outward too: have you seen someone else handle a similar situation, or speak more kindly about themselves, and found that it worked for them? If they could do it, what might that suggest is possible for you?

5

A Kinder, Truer Line

Write one sentence that honours your strength, learning, or the care involved — something you actually believe.
Write it in the present tense. "I am someone who..." carries a different weight to "I used to be..." or "I will try to...". Present tense anchors the new narrative in who you are now, not who you were or might become.
For example — Margaret's kinder line

Old narrative: "Nobody really listens to me. Speaking up is not worth it."

Kinder, truer line: "I am someone whose perspective has value, and I am learning to offer it to people who are ready to hear it."

It does not have to feel triumphant — it just needs to be honest, a little gentler, and written in the present tense.
If you find this difficult, try beginning with: "I am someone who..." or "One thing I know to be true about myself is..."
6

Tiny Support Action

What small behaviour backs up your kinder line? It should take no more than ten minutes.
For example — Margaret's tiny action

Action: "Share one opinion at Thursday's team catch-up, even briefly."

When: "Thursday morning — I will prepare one sentence the night before so I feel ready."

Something concrete and small — write a note, make a call, say one thing, take a short walk, tidy one corner.
Being specific makes it far more likely to happen.
7

Rain Practice

Lighten the mood. Pick a small task you resist and add something that shifts how it feels.

The narrative you carry about a task shapes how you experience it. Adding music, humour, or a small ritual to something you resist can genuinely shift your mood — a playlist, a funny mug, a self-imposed rule. Small tweaks, real change.

For example — Margaret's rain practice

The task: "Replying to difficult emails — I put it off for days."

What I will add: "I make a good coffee first, put on an upbeat playlist, and give myself exactly 20 minutes. I call it my Power Half Hour even though it is only 20 minutes — the name makes me smile."

Music, humour, a ritual, a small reward, a timer, a friend — whatever makes it a little lighter.
8

Voice Bank

We all carry helpful and unhelpful inner voices. Name the ones that support you — and gently retire one that does not.
For example — Margaret's voice bank

Helpful lines: "My perspective matters even if not everyone hears it." / "I have navigated harder things than a difficult conversation." / "Sarah always listens — I can practise being heard with her."

To retire: "Nobody listens to me." → Replaced with: "The right people do hear me, and I am finding more of them."

These might be phrases someone said to you, something you return to, or something you simply know to be true.

Seven-Day Tracker

Mark each day you practised your kinder line or took your tiny action. Small and consistent is all that matters.
Tick a day whenever you returned to your kinder line, took your tiny action, or simply noticed the old narrative and chose a different response — however briefly.
